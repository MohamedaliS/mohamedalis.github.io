3.1 Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Define population, sample, parameter, and statistic precisely.
  2. Explain why samples rather than populations are usually studied.
  3. Define sampling error and explain why it is unavoidable.
  4. Describe how sample size affects the reliability of an estimate.
  5. Distinguish sampling error from non-sampling error.

3.2 Introduction

Before any statistical analysis can be interpreted, four terms need to be understood exactly: population, sample, parameter, and statistic. These words are used loosely in everyday conversation, “the population of users who clicked the link,” “a statistic showing customer satisfaction”, but in statistics they have precise meanings that determine what kind of claim you are entitled to make.

This chapter defines each term, explains why the distinction between them matters, and introduces sampling error: the unavoidable gap between what a sample shows and what the population actually looks like.

3.3 Population

In statistics, a population is the complete set of individuals, objects, or events that a study is concerned with. The definition of the population comes from the research question, not from any property of the data collected.

Some examples clarify the range:

  • A study of customer satisfaction at a retail chain might define the population as all customers who made a purchase in the last 12 months.
  • A quality control analysis might define the population as all units produced by a particular machine on a particular shift.
  • A study of graduate outcomes might define the population as all students who graduated from a given programme over a ten-year period.

Populations can be finite (a fixed number of members) or infinite (ongoing processes, future events, all possible measurements under a given condition). They can be large or small. What makes them the population is that the research question is about them, they are the group to which the conclusions are intended to apply.

This definition has an important consequence. If you collect data from every member of your population, you have a census. A census is rare. Most populations are too large, too dispersed, or too dynamic to measure completely, which is why most statistical work relies on samples.

3.4 Sample

A sample is a subset of the population, selected for actual observation or measurement. The sample is what you have; the population is what you want to know about.

The value of a sample depends on how well it represents the population. A sample of 500 customers drawn randomly from your complete customer database is far more useful than a sample of 500 customers who happened to fill in a feedback card on a particular Tuesday, even if both samples are the same size. The first sample is likely to represent the full range of customers. The second is likely to over-represent certain types.

How the sample is selected, the sampling method, is one of the most consequential decisions in any study. Chapter 4 covers this in detail.

3.5 Parameters and statistics

The terms parameter and statistic describe numbers that summarise a group, but which group matters.

A parameter is a numerical summary of a population. The mean income of all adults in a country, the proportion of all products from a factory that are defective, the standard deviation of all exam scores from an entire cohort, these are parameters. Parameters are usually unknown, because the complete population data is usually unavailable.

A statistic (singular) is a numerical summary of a sample. The mean income of 1,200 adults surveyed, the proportion of defective products in a batch of 200 inspected items, the standard deviation of exam scores from 150 students sampled from a cohort, these are statistics. Statistics are known, because they are calculated directly from the data collected.

The goal of inferential statistics is to use statistics to estimate parameters. The sample mean estimates the population mean. The sample proportion estimates the population proportion. Because the sample is only a subset of the population, the estimate will not be exact, and that gap has a name.

3.5.1 Notation convention

A standard notation makes the distinction concrete:

Population (parameter) Sample (statistic)
Mean μ (mu) x̄ (x-bar)
Standard deviation σ (sigma) s
Proportion π or p p̂ (p-hat)
Size N n

Parameters are typically denoted by Greek letters. Sample statistics use Roman letters or letters with a hat (^). When you see μ, you are being told something about a population. When you see x̄, you are being told something about a sample.

3.6 Sampling error

Sampling error is the difference between a sample statistic and the corresponding population parameter. It arises not from mistakes in measurement or calculation but from the simple fact that a sample is not the whole population.

If the average weekly revenue across all 200 branches of a retail chain is £48,200 (the parameter μ), but a random sample of 30 branches yields an average of £47,650 (the statistic x̄), the sampling error is £48,200 − £47,650 = £550. No one made an error. The sample just happened to include slightly lower-revenue branches than the full population average would suggest.

This matters in three ways:

Sampling error is unavoidable. Any sample will differ from the population to some degree. The goal is not to eliminate sampling error but to keep it manageable and to measure it honestly.

Sampling error varies with sample size. Larger samples tend to produce smaller sampling errors. This is because larger samples are less likely to deviate substantially from the population as a whole. The precise relationship is captured by the standard error, which is covered in Chapter 17.

Sampling error is random. If you drew many different samples from the same population, the statistics would vary around the true parameter. Some samples would over-estimate, some would under-estimate, but the average of many sample statistics would be close to the parameter. This property is the foundation of confidence intervals and hypothesis testing.

3.6.1 Sampling error versus non-sampling error

Sampling error should not be confused with other sources of inaccuracy, which are collected under the term non-sampling error. Non-sampling errors include:

  • Measurement error: the question was poorly worded, the instrument was miscalibrated, or responses were recorded incorrectly.
  • Coverage error: the sampling frame (the list from which the sample was drawn) did not include all members of the population, so certain groups had no chance of being selected.
  • Non-response bias: the people who agreed to participate differ systematically from those who did not.
  • Processing error: data entry or coding errors introduced after the data was collected.

Non-sampling errors, unlike sampling error, do not necessarily decrease as sample size grows. Surveying more people from a biased sampling frame gives you a larger biased sample, it does not make the sample more representative.

3.7 Worked example

Code 
set.seed(42)

# Simulate a population of 5,000 customer transaction values (in £)
# Population mean (parameter) is known here because we are constructing the
# population, in practice it would not be.
population <- rnorm(5000, mean = 85, sd = 22)
mu <- mean(population)

# Draw 500 samples of size n = 30 and record each sample mean
sample_means_30 <- replicate(500, mean(sample(population, 30)))

# Draw 500 samples of size n = 100
sample_means_100 <- replicate(500, mean(sample(population, 100)))

# Compare
data.frame(
  sample_mean = c(sample_means_30, sample_means_100),
  size = factor(rep(c("n = 30", "n = 100"), each = 500))
) |>
  ggplot(aes(x = sample_mean, fill = size)) +
  geom_histogram(bins = 40, alpha = 0.7, position = "identity") +
  geom_vline(xintercept = mu, linetype = "dashed", colour = "#C0392B", linewidth = 0.8) +
  scale_fill_manual(values = c("#1B3A5C", "#E8A020")) +
  labs(
    title    = "Sampling error shrinks as sample size increases",
    subtitle = sprintf("True population mean (dashed line) = £%.2f", mu),
    x        = "Sample mean (£)",
    y        = "Count of samples",
    fill     = "Sample size",
    caption  = "500 samples drawn at each size from a simulated population of 5,000 transactions."
  ) +
  theme_dmm()

The chart shows that both distributions of sample means are centred near the true population mean, neither sample size produces systematically biased estimates. But the spread of sample means is considerably wider for n = 30 than for n = 100. Larger samples cluster more tightly around the true value. This is what it means to say that larger samples reduce sampling error.

Code 
data.frame(
  "Sample size"  = c(30, 100),
  "Mean of sample means" = c(mean(sample_means_30), mean(sample_means_100)),
  "SD of sample means"   = c(sd(sample_means_30), sd(sample_means_100))
) |>
  dmm_table(caption = "Effect of sample size on sampling error (500 simulated samples each)", digits = 2)
Effect of sample size on sampling error (500 simulated samples each)
Sample.size Mean.of.sample.means SD.of.sample.means
30 84.89 4.03
100 84.65 2.14

The standard deviation of the sample means, called the standard error, falls substantially when the sample size quadruples from 30 to 100. This illustrates a key result: the standard error decreases in proportion to the square root of the sample size, not the sample size itself. To halve the sampling error, you need to quadruple the sample size.

3.8 Summary

A population is the complete set of cases a study is concerned with. A sample is the subset actually observed. A parameter is a numerical summary of the population; a statistic is a numerical summary of the sample. Inferential statistics uses statistics to estimate parameters.

Sampling error is the inevitable difference between a sample statistic and the true population parameter. It arises from the randomness of the sampling process, not from mistakes. It decreases as sample size increases but never reaches zero unless a complete census is taken. Sampling error is distinct from non-sampling error, which can affect results regardless of sample size and which cannot be reduced simply by collecting more observations from the same flawed process.

3.10 Exercises

  1. A supermarket wants to know the average amount spent per visit by all customers last month. Define the population, state what a parameter would be in this context, and explain why a census would be impractical.

  2. A sample of 50 employees is drawn from a workforce of 800. The sample has a mean age of 34.2 years. Is 34.2 a parameter or a statistic? What symbol would be used to represent it?

  3. Two analysts estimate the same population mean using different sample sizes: one uses n = 40, the other uses n = 160. If both use random sampling, which estimate is likely to be closer to the true parameter, and why?

  4. A company sends a customer satisfaction survey to 1,000 randomly selected customers, but only 120 respond. What type of error is the main concern here, and why can it not be reduced simply by sending the survey to more people?

  5. In the worked example, why are both distributions of sample means centred near the true population mean of £85, even though one uses samples of only 30 observations?
