17.1 Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student should be able to:

  1. Explain what a sampling distribution is and why it matters for inference.
  2. State the central limit theorem and its conditions.
  3. Calculate and interpret the standard error of the mean.
  4. Explain the relationship between sample size and the precision of an estimate.
  5. Describe why the central limit theorem makes normal-based inference broadly applicable.

17.2 Introduction

The gap between description and inference was first identified in Chapter 02. Description summarises the data at hand. Inference draws conclusions about a larger population from a sample. The bridge between them is the sampling distribution: the distribution of a statistic, such as the sample mean, across all possible samples of a given size from a population.

Understanding the sampling distribution is essential because it tells us how much a sample statistic is likely to deviate from the corresponding population parameter simply by chance. This uncertainty, quantified as the standard error, is the basis of confidence intervals, hypothesis tests, and almost every inferential procedure in the remaining chapters.

The dataset for this unit is a population of 2,000 warehouse orders, with variables including fulfilment hours, weight, category, and distance. We treat it as the full population and repeatedly draw samples to illustrate what sampling distributions look like in practice.

17.3 What is a sampling distribution?

Suppose the true population mean fulfilment time for all 2,000 orders is \(\mu\). We do not know this in real work, but here we can compute it directly.

Code 
library(dplyr)

pop_mean <- mean(df$fulfilment_hours)
pop_sd   <- sd(df$fulfilment_hours)
pop_n    <- nrow(df)

params <- data.frame(
  Parameter = c("Population mean (mu)", "Population SD (sigma)",
                "Population size (N)"),
  Value     = round(c(pop_mean, pop_sd, pop_n), 2)
)

knitr::kable(params,
  col.names = c("Parameter", "Value"),
  caption   = "Table 17.1: True population parameters for fulfilment hours") |>
  kableExtra::kable_styling(bootstrap_options = c("striped"),
                             full_width = FALSE)
Table 17.1: True population parameters for fulfilment hours
Parameter Value
Population mean (mu) 12.16
Population SD (sigma) 3.39
Population size (N) 2000.00

Now suppose a researcher observes only a sample of 40 orders and computes the sample mean. Their value will differ from \(\mu\) — that difference is sampling error. If a second researcher independently takes a different sample of 40, they will obtain a slightly different mean. The sampling distribution of the mean is the distribution of all such sample means across all possible samples of size n.

17.4 Demonstrating the sampling distribution

Code 
set.seed(42)
sample_means_n30  <- replicate(1000, mean(sample(df$fulfilment_hours, 30)))
sample_means_n100 <- replicate(1000, mean(sample(df$fulfilment_hours, 100)))

samp_summary <- data.frame(
  Sample_size = c(30, 100),
  Mean_of_means = round(c(mean(sample_means_n30),
                           mean(sample_means_n100)), 3),
  SD_of_means   = round(c(sd(sample_means_n30),
                           sd(sample_means_n100)), 3),
  Theoretical_SE = round(c(pop_sd / sqrt(30),
                            pop_sd / sqrt(100)), 3)
)

knitr::kable(samp_summary,
  col.names = c("n", "Mean of sample means",
                "SD of sample means (observed SE)",
                "Theoretical SE (sigma/sqrt(n))"),
  caption   = "Table 17.2: Sampling distributions for two sample sizes") |>
  kableExtra::kable_styling(bootstrap_options = c("striped", "hover"),
                             full_width = FALSE)
Table 17.2: Sampling distributions for two sample sizes
n Mean of sample means SD of sample means (observed SE) Theoretical SE (sigma/sqrt(n))
30 12.137 0.629 0.619
100 12.136 0.332 0.339

Two features are visible in Table 17.2. First, the mean of all sample means is very close to the population mean: sample means cluster around the true value. Second, as sample size increases, the spread of the sample means decreases. The standard deviation of the sampling distribution is the standard error.

17.5 The standard error

The standard error of the mean is the standard deviation of the sampling distribution. For a population with standard deviation \(\sigma\) and sample size \(n\):

\[SE_{\bar{x}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\]

In practice, \(\sigma\) is usually unknown and is estimated by the sample standard deviation \(s\):

\[\widehat{SE}_{\bar{x}} = \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\]

The standard error decreases as sample size increases. To halve the standard error, the sample size must be quadrupled.

17.6 The central limit theorem

The central limit theorem (CLT) states that, regardless of the shape of the population distribution, the sampling distribution of the sample mean approaches a normal distribution as the sample size increases.

More precisely: if random samples of size \(n\) are drawn from a population with mean \(\mu\) and standard deviation \(\sigma\), then for sufficiently large \(n\):

\[\bar{X} \sim N\!\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)\]

The practical implication is that normal-based inference is valid even when the population is not normally distributed, provided the sample is large enough.

The population distribution is right-skewed. The sampling distributions for n = 30 and n = 100 are both approximately bell-shaped and centred on the population mean. The n = 100 distribution is narrower, reflecting greater precision.

17.7 How large is “sufficiently large”?

The CLT applies faster (with smaller n) when the population is already approximately symmetric. For moderately skewed populations, n ≥ 30 is a commonly used rule of thumb. For heavily skewed distributions or those with extreme outliers, larger samples are needed.

17.8 The square root law

\[SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\]

This relationship has a practical implication for survey design and quality control: precision improves with sample size, but at a diminishing rate.

Table 17.3: Standard error at different sample sizes
Sample size n Standard error (estimated)
10 1.072
25 0.678
50 0.480
100 0.339
200 0.240
400 0.170
1000 0.107

Doubling from n = 50 to n = 100 reduces the SE by roughly 30%. Doubling from n = 400 to n = 800 produces the same proportional reduction, but costs four times as many additional observations. This diminishing return shapes the cost-benefit analysis of sample size decisions.

17.9 Summary

A sampling distribution is the distribution of a sample statistic across all possible samples of a given size. The sampling distribution of the mean has a mean equal to the population mean and a standard deviation equal to the standard error, \(\sigma / \sqrt{n}\).

The central limit theorem guarantees that this sampling distribution approaches normality as n increases, regardless of the shape of the population. This is the theoretical foundation for all normal-based confidence intervals and hypothesis tests covered in the remaining chapters.

17.11 Exercises

  1. Using Table 17.2, the observed SE for n = 30 is close to but not exactly equal to the theoretical SE. Why might they differ slightly?

  2. A population has mean 50 and standard deviation 12. What is the standard error of the mean for samples of size 36? Of size 144? How does the SE change when the sample size quadruples?

  3. The population of fulfilment hours is right-skewed. Looking at Figure 17.2, at what sample size do the sampling distributions begin to look approximately normal? What does this tell you about applying the CLT to this population?

  4. A manager wants to estimate the mean delivery time to within 2 minutes with a standard error of 1 minute. If the population standard deviation is 8 minutes, what sample size is required?

  5. A colleague says: “The CLT says that if we take a large enough sample, the data will be normally distributed.” Is this correct? What does the CLT actually say?

## The central limit theorem

The **central limit theorem** (CLT) states that, regardless of the shape of the population distribution, the sampling distribution of the sample mean approaches a normal distribution as the sample size increases.

More precisely: if random samples of size $n$ are drawn from a population with mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$, then for sufficiently large $n$:

$$\bar{X} \sim N\!\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)$$

The practical implication is that normal-based inference is valid even when the population is not normally distributed, provided the sample is large enough.

```{r}
#| label: ch17-clt-plot
#| echo: false
#| fig-height: 4.2
#| fig-cap: "Figure 17.1: The CLT in action — population vs sampling distributions"
library(gridExtra)

pop_plot <- ggplot(df, aes(x = fulfilment_hours)) +
  geom_histogram(bins = 40, fill = "#1B3A5C",
                 colour = "white", alpha = 0.85) +
  labs(x = "Fulfilment hours", y = "Count",
       title = "Population distribution (N = 2,000)\nRight-skewed") +
  theme_dmm()

samp_df <- data.frame(
  xbar = c(sample_means_n30, sample_means_n100),
  n    = factor(rep(c("n = 30", "n = 100"), each = 1000),
                levels = c("n = 30", "n = 100"))
)
samp_plot <- ggplot(samp_df, aes(x = xbar, fill = n)) +
  geom_histogram(bins = 35, alpha = 0.75,
                 position = "identity", colour = "white") +
  scale_fill_manual(values = c("#E8A020", "#C0392B")) +
  geom_vline(xintercept = pop_mean, linetype = "dashed",
             colour = "#1B3A5C", linewidth = 0.9) +
  labs(x = "Sample mean", y = "Count",
       title = "Sampling distributions (1,000 samples each)\nApproach normal regardless of population shape",
       fill = "Sample size") +
  theme_dmm()

gridExtra::grid.arrange(pop_plot, samp_plot, ncol = 2)
```

```{r}
sizes_df <- do.call(rbind, lapply(c(5, 15, 30, 100), function(n) {
```
