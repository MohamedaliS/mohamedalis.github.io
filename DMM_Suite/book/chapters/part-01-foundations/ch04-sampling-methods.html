4.1 Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Explain what a sampling frame is and why it matters.
  2. Describe simple random sampling and how to implement it.
  3. Describe systematic, stratified, cluster, and convenience sampling.
  4. Compare probability and non-probability sampling methods.
  5. Identify the risk of bias in non-probability samples.
  6. Select an appropriate sampling method for a given management scenario.

4.2 Introduction

How you select a sample determines what you can legitimately say about the population. The most sophisticated statistical analysis cannot rescue a poorly drawn sample. A result derived from a biased sample is not an imprecise estimate, it is a systematically wrong one, and more data from the same source will not fix it.

This chapter covers the most common sampling methods, the conditions under which each is appropriate, and the errors that arise when the method does not match the question.

4.3 The sampling frame

Before selecting a sample, you need a sampling frame: a list or organised representation of the population from which the sample will be drawn. If the population is all active customers, the sampling frame might be the customer database. If the population is all products manufactured last week, the frame might be the production log.

The sampling frame should cover the population completely. When it does not, coverage error results: some members of the population have no chance of being selected, which introduces systematic bias. A telephone survey conducted using landline numbers, for example, automatically excludes everyone who only has a mobile phone, a group that differs in potentially important ways from the rest of the population.

Identifying a complete and accurate sampling frame is often the most difficult practical challenge in data collection.

4.4 Probability sampling methods

In probability sampling, every member of the population has a known, non-zero probability of being selected. This property is what makes inferential conclusions justifiable, without it, you cannot calculate sampling error or construct valid confidence intervals.

4.4.1 Simple random sampling

In simple random sampling (SRS), every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected, and every possible sample of size n has an equal chance of being the one drawn.

In practice, SRS is implemented by assigning a number to each member of the sampling frame and then using a random number generator (or statistical software) to select the sample. In R:

Code 
set.seed(101)
population_ids <- 1:500   # a frame of 500 employees
sample_ids     <- sample(population_ids, size = 50)
head(sample_ids, 10)
 [1] 329 313 430  95 209 442 351 317 444 315

SRS is the conceptual baseline against which all other methods are compared. Its main limitation is practical: it requires a complete sampling frame, and when the population is large and geographically dispersed, reaching randomly selected members can be expensive or logistically difficult.

4.4.2 Systematic sampling

Systematic sampling selects every k-th member of the sampling frame, where k is the population size divided by the desired sample size. The starting point is chosen at random from the first k members.

If a frame contains 1,000 customers and a sample of 100 is needed, k = 10. A starting point between 1 and 10 is chosen at random, say, 7, and then every 10th member is selected: 7, 17, 27, 37, and so on.

Systematic sampling is convenient and produces results close to SRS when the frame has no periodic pattern. It becomes problematic if the frame is arranged in a cycle that aligns with the sampling interval. A list of store opening shifts ordered by day of the week, sampled every 7th entry, would consistently draw from the same day.

4.4.3 Stratified sampling

Stratified sampling divides the population into non-overlapping subgroups (strata) based on a characteristic relevant to the study, and then draws a separate random sample from each stratum.

The advantage is that it guarantees representation of each subgroup and can produce more precise estimates than SRS when the strata differ substantially from each other.

Proportionate stratified sampling draws from each stratum in proportion to its size in the population. If women make up 60% of the workforce, 60% of the sample comes from the female stratum.

Disproportionate stratified sampling deliberately over-samples smaller strata to ensure adequate representation. This is useful when you want to make comparisons between groups of very different sizes, for example, comparing satisfaction scores across three product lines where one line accounts for 80% of sales and another for only 5%.

Code 
# Proportionate stratified sample from a workforce with three departments
set.seed(202)

workforce <- data.frame(
  id         = 1:600,
  department = rep(c("Operations", "Sales", "Finance"), times = c(300, 200, 100))
)

target_n <- 60  # total sample

# Sample 10% from each stratum
library(dplyr)
stratified_sample <- workforce |>
  group_by(department) |>
  slice_sample(prop = 0.10) |>
  ungroup()

table(stratified_sample$department)

   Finance Operations      Sales 
        10         30         20

4.4.4 Cluster sampling

Cluster sampling divides the population into clusters, typically defined by geography or administrative groupings, selects a random sample of clusters, and then includes all (or a random sub-sample of) members within the chosen clusters.

A retailer wanting to survey customers across 120 stores might find it impractical to sample from all stores. Instead, they randomly select 20 stores and survey all customers who shop there during a defined period. The 20 stores are the clusters.

Cluster sampling is more economical than SRS for geographically dispersed populations but typically less statistically efficient: if people within clusters are similar to each other (as neighbours or regular customers of the same store often are), the effective sample diversity is lower than the headcount suggests.

4.5 Non-probability sampling

In non-probability sampling, members of the population are not selected according to known, random probabilities. This makes it impossible to calculate sampling error formally, which limits what can legitimately be concluded.

Non-probability samples are widely used in practice, particularly in exploratory research, user testing, and settings where probability sampling is simply not feasible. They are not inherently worthless, but the conclusions drawn from them should be proportionately cautious.

4.5.1 Convenience sampling

A convenience sample includes whoever is easiest to reach: shoppers in a particular location, respondents who click on a survey link, students in a class. It is the most common type of non-probability sample and the one most vulnerable to bias.

The problem is not the method itself but the conclusions it is used to support. If a product team surveys a convenience sample of 200 users and concludes “our users prefer Feature A,” the honest statement is: “of the 200 people who responded to this survey, most preferred Feature A.” Whether that reflects the broader user base is unknown.

4.5.2 Purposive sampling

Purposive sampling (also called judgement sampling) selects participants based on the researcher’s judgement that they are representative or informative. An industry analyst interviewing senior executives from leading firms is using purposive sampling. It can be appropriate in qualitative research and when specific expertise is being sought, but it cannot support quantitative generalisations.

4.5.3 Snowball sampling

Snowball sampling starts with a small number of participants who then refer other potential participants. It is useful when the target population is hard to reach, such as members of a professional network or participants in niche markets, but the resulting sample is unlikely to be representative of the broader population.

4.5.4 Quota sampling

Quota sampling sets targets for the composition of the sample (similar to stratified sampling) but fills those targets non-randomly. An interviewer might be instructed to find 50 men and 50 women, but left to approach whoever is convenient. Because the selection within each quota is not random, the formal guarantees of stratified sampling do not apply.

4.6 Choosing a sampling method

The choice of sampling method depends on several factors:

Factor Consideration
Do you need formal statistical inference? If yes, probability sampling is required.
Is a complete sampling frame available? If no, probability sampling may not be possible.
How large and dispersed is the population? Large/dispersed populations may require cluster or systematic approaches.
Are there important subgroups to represent? Stratification may be advisable.
What resources are available? Census and SRS can be expensive; cluster and systematic are more economical.
Is the research exploratory? Convenience or purposive sampling may be acceptable if conclusions are qualified.

4.7 Worked example

Note

Scenario. A regional health service wants to survey staff satisfaction across 40 hospitals of varying sizes. Total clinical staff: approximately 22,000.

Option 1, Simple random sampling. Assign a number to each of the 22,000 staff members and draw 1,100 (5%) randomly. Statistically ideal but operationally difficult: reaching specific named individuals across 40 institutions requires coordination across HR systems.

Option 2, Stratified sampling by hospital size. Divide the 40 hospitals into small, medium, and large strata. Draw a proportionate random sample from each stratum’s staff list. This ensures that the sample represents the range of institutional settings and allows comparisons between hospital sizes.

Option 3, Cluster sampling. Randomly select 10 of the 40 hospitals and survey all clinical staff within those 10. Less statistically efficient (staff within a hospital are likely to share similar experiences), but logistically far simpler.

For a survey intended to produce nationally generalisable estimates and allow subgroup comparisons, stratified sampling is the strongest choice. If budget constrains data collection to a few sites, cluster sampling with 10 randomly chosen hospitals is defensible, provided conclusions are qualified accordingly.

4.8 Summary

A sampling frame is the list from which a sample is drawn; gaps in the frame produce coverage error. Probability sampling methods, simple random, systematic, stratified, and cluster, each give every population member a known chance of selection, which is what makes formal statistical inference possible. Non-probability methods, convenience, purposive, snowball, quota, are more common in practice but do not support formal inference because sampling probabilities are unknown.

The choice of sampling method should be made before data collection, guided by the research question, the available frame, practical constraints, and the conclusions the analysis is expected to support.

4.10 Exercises

  1. A supermarket wants to survey 200 customers about their shopping experience. Describe how you would implement each of the following: (a) simple random sampling, (b) systematic sampling, (c) stratified sampling by time of day. What are the main practical advantages of each?

  2. A researcher surveys university students by standing outside the library and asking whoever walks by. What type of sampling is this? What are the main risks for any conclusions drawn from this sample?

  3. A manufacturer wants to test the quality of output from five production lines. She randomly selects two of the five lines and tests every item produced on those lines over a one-day period. What type of sampling is this? What bias might result?

  4. An online retailer has customer records for 3.2 million people. They want a sample of 5,000. Describe the steps for implementing simple random sampling in practice.

  5. A consulting firm proposes quota sampling to study attitudes toward a new policy among three industry groups. The quotas match the industry groups’ sizes in the population. Explain why this is not equivalent to proportionate stratified sampling, and what additional step would make it so.
