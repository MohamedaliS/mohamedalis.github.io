1.1 Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student should be able to:

  1. Define statistics and distinguish between its two principal branches.
  2. Explain why managers rely on data rather than intuition alone.
  3. Describe the basic cycle that connects a question to a statistical answer.
  4. Identify realistic management situations in which statistical thinking applies.
  5. Recognise the limits of what statistics can and cannot do.

1.2 Introduction

Every manager makes decisions under uncertainty. A retailer does not know how many customers will arrive on a given Saturday. A hospital administrator cannot predict how many beds will be required next month. A product manager launching a new feature has no means of reading the market in advance. What statistics offers is not certainty. It offers a disciplined way of reducing uncertainty and of measuring what remains.

This chapter addresses a basic question: what is statistics, and why does it matter to a person whose work is to manage an organisation rather than to study numbers for their own sake? The answer has two parts. First, statistics is a set of tools for collecting, summarising, and drawing conclusions from data. Second, and more important for the practitioner, it is a way of thinking, one that separates what the evidence actually shows from what one assumes or hopes it shows.

1.3 What statistics is

The word statistics is used in at least three senses. It refers to a discipline, a field of scholarly inquiry. It refers to methods, the procedures employed to collect and analyse data. And it refers to numbers themselves, particularly numbers that summarise a dataset, such as an average or a percentage.

All three senses are relevant, but the most useful definition for a working manager is this: statistics is the science of learning from data under conditions of uncertainty.

The phrase under conditions of uncertainty is what distinguishes statistics from arithmetic. Adding up this quarter’s sales figures is arithmetic. Deciding whether those figures convey something reliable about next quarter’s demand is a statistical question.

1.3.1 The two branches

Statistics divides into two broad areas: descriptive statistics and inferential statistics.

Descriptive statistics summarises and organises data that one already possesses. Calculating the average customer satisfaction score across 500 survey responses, or drawing a bar chart showing sales by region, are both descriptive activities. Nothing is being predicted or inferred. Existing information is simply placed in a form that is easier to comprehend.

Inferential statistics goes further. It uses data from a smaller group, a sample, to draw conclusions about a larger group, a population. If 200 customers are surveyed and those results are used to estimate how all customers feel, an inference is being made. That inference involves uncertainty, and statistics provides the tools to measure and communicate how much uncertainty is present.

The distinction matters in practice. Descriptive statistics reports what occurred in the data one holds. Inferential statistics assists in judging what is probably true more broadly, and how confident one should be in that judgement.

1.4 Why managers need statistical thinking

Managers have always worked with numbers. What has changed is the volume of data available and the expectations placed on decisions. Spreadsheets, customer databases, website analytics, and operational systems now produce more data than any individual can read. The question is no longer whether data are available. It is whether those data can be interpreted with honesty and rigour.

1.4.1 The problem with intuition

Human intuition is well suited to many activities: reading people, navigating social situations, making rapid judgements in familiar contexts. It is poorly suited to detecting patterns in large datasets, estimating probabilities, and recognising when a trend is real rather than accidental.

Several well-documented biases affect how people naturally interpret data, as discussed at length in Kahneman (2011).

Availability bias leads one to overweight information that comes to mind readily. A dramatic customer complaint, for instance, tends to receive disproportionate attention relative to the broader pattern of feedback.

Confirmation bias leads one to attend more closely to evidence that supports existing beliefs and to discount evidence that challenges them.

Small-sample thinking leads one to treat a small number of observations as more informative than they actually are. If the last three product launches succeeded, confidence in the fourth tends to be high, even when three observations are nowhere near sufficient to establish a reliable pattern.

Statistical thinking does not eliminate these biases. It provides a framework for checking conclusions against evidence, rather than allowing evidence to be bent to fit conclusions.

1.4.2 What managers actually do with statistics

The applications of statistics in management are not abstract. They appear in routine work across every organisational function, as Anderson et al. (2020) illustrates with extensive business case material.

Operations and logistics. A distribution centre manager uses historical data on delivery times to set realistic expectations and to detect deterioration in performance before it becomes a crisis.

Marketing. A marketing team tests two versions of an email subject line, sending each to half of a randomly selected list. A statistical test determines whether the difference in open rates reflects a real difference or merely random variation.

Finance. A finance analyst reviewing branch performance needs to know whether one branch’s lower profit margin reflects a genuine structural problem or simply the short duration of the comparison period.

Human resources. An HR director examining pay data across the organisation wants to determine whether apparent differences in pay between groups reflect experience and role, or whether they indicate something that warrants further investigation.

In each case, the statistical question has the same underlying structure: is what is visible in the data a reliable signal, or is it noise?

1.6 The basic cycle: from question to answer

Statistical work tends to follow a recognisable sequence. Understanding this sequence helps managers commission and evaluate analysis, even when they are not conducting the calculations themselves.

1.6.1 Step 1: Define the question

Good statistical work begins with a clear, specific question. “How are sales?” is not a statistical question. “Did average weekly sales in the northern region increase between the first and second quarter of this financial year, and if so, by how much?” is one. The specificity matters because vague questions produce unfocused analysis that is difficult to interpret and easy to misuse.

1.6.2 Step 2: Collect data

Once the question is established, data that can actually answer it must be obtained. This involves decisions about what to measure, where to collect information, how to record it, and how to ensure accuracy. Poor data collection undermines everything that follows. A statistically sophisticated analysis of badly collected data produces confident but incorrect answers.

1.6.3 Step 3: Describe the data

Before drawing any conclusions, one must examine what is available. Summary statistics and visualisations such as histograms and scatter plots reveal the basic shape of the data. This step frequently brings to light errors, outliers, or patterns that alter the direction of the analysis.

1.6.4 Step 4: Analyse and draw conclusions

Depending on the question, this step may involve calculating a confidence interval, running a hypothesis test, or building a regression model. The output is not a final answer but a probabilistic statement: given these data, here is what the evidence supports, and here is how certain one can be.

1.6.5 Step 5: Communicate and decide

Statistical conclusions must be translated into plain language before they can be useful to decision-makers. A p-value conveys little to most managers. What they need to know is whether the evidence is strong enough to act on, what the margin of error is, and what would follow if the conclusion proved to be incorrect.

1.7 What statistics cannot do

It is worth being precise about the limits of statistics, because unrealistic expectations lead either to over-reliance on the method or to cynicism about it.

Statistics does not prove things. It assesses the weight of evidence. A well-designed study that produces a strong result gives good reason to believe something. It does not make that something certain.

Statistics does not remove the need for judgement. A statistical test may indicate that the difference between two approaches is statistically significant. It cannot indicate whether that difference is large enough to matter in practice, or whether the cost of acting on it is justified.

Statistics cannot correct for bad data. If the data collected do not measure what was intended, or if the sample was not representative of the population of interest, no degree of analytical sophistication will rescue the result.

Statistics does not establish causation on its own. A correlation between two variables does not by itself establish that one caused the other. Causation requires additional evidence beyond the correlation alone.

These limits are not reasons to distrust statistics. They are reasons to use it carefully, to read results critically, and to treat statistical conclusions as one important input among several, rather than as the final word.

1.8 Worked example

Note

Scenario. A regional manager at a coffee chain observes that three branches in her area reported fewer customer complaints last month than in the previous month. She concludes that the service improvement initiative launched six weeks earlier is working, and recommends extending it to all 47 branches.

This chain of reasoning is understandable but contains several problems that statistical thinking would identify.

Problem 1: Sample size. Three branches is a very small number. Random variation alone can easily produce a month in which complaints happen to be lower in three out of forty-seven locations.

Problem 2: No comparison group. If complaints fell across all branches rather than only in the three that received the initiative, something else may explain the decline. Perhaps it was a quieter month overall.

Problem 3: Causation assumed from correlation. Even if complaints genuinely fell in the three branches, one cannot conclude that the initiative caused the fall without ruling out other explanations.

A more defensible approach would be to compare complaint rates across all branches, assess whether the three treated branches differ from the rest, and conduct a formal test before drawing conclusions. Chapters 20 through 23 cover the tools required for exactly this kind of analysis.

1.9 Summary

Statistics is the science of learning from data under conditions of uncertainty. It divides into descriptive statistics, which summarises data already collected, and inferential statistics, which uses sample data to draw conclusions about a broader population.

Managers require statistical thinking because human intuition is unreliable when dealing with large datasets, probability, and patterns that may be real or may be random. Every business function regularly faces questions that can only be answered well with data and a framework for interpreting it honestly.

The basic cycle of statistical work runs from defining a clear question, through collecting and describing data, through analysis, to communication and decision. Statistics does not produce certainty, does not remove the need for judgement, and cannot correct bad data or establish causation on its own.

1.11 Exercises

  1. A colleague says: “We received three complaints about the new product last week, so something is clearly wrong.” What statistical concern does this statement raise, and how would you respond?

  2. Identify one decision made in the past month at an organisation with which you are familiar. Was data used? If so, was the analysis descriptive, inferential, or both? If data were not used, what information would have assisted the decision?

  3. A report states: “Regions that spent more on training had higher sales.” A manager concludes that training causes higher sales. What is wrong with this conclusion, and what additional evidence would be required to support it?

  4. List three questions from your own field or area of interest that could be addressed statistically. For each, note whether the question is primarily descriptive or inferential.

  5. A chief executive reads a summary reporting that 78 per cent of employees are “satisfied” with their work. She says: “Good, most people are happy.” What questions should she ask before accepting this conclusion?

## Exercises

1. A colleague says: "We received three complaints about the new product last week, so something is clearly wrong." What statistical concern does this statement raise, and how would you respond?

2. Identify one decision made in the past month at an organisation with which you are familiar. Was data used? If so, was the analysis descriptive, inferential, or both? If data were not used, what information would have assisted the decision?

3. A report states: "Regions that spent more on training had higher sales." A manager concludes that training causes higher sales. What is wrong with this conclusion, and what additional evidence would be required to support it?

4. List three questions from your own field or area of interest that could be addressed statistically. For each, note whether the question is primarily descriptive or inferential.

5. A chief executive reads a summary reporting that 78 per cent of employees are "satisfied" with their work. She says: "Good, most people are happy." What questions should she ask before accepting this conclusion?
