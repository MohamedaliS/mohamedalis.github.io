2.1 Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  1. Distinguish clearly between descriptive and inferential statistics.
  2. Describe the role of each branch in a complete analysis.
  3. Recognise which branch is being used in a given report or summary.
  4. Explain why moving from description to inference introduces uncertainty.
  5. Identify common errors that arise from treating descriptive results as if they were inferential conclusions.

2.2 Introduction

The distinction between descriptive and inferential statistics is one of the most important in the field, and also one of the most frequently blurred in practice. Reports that describe what happened in a dataset are often written as though they prove something about the wider world. Summaries of a sample are presented as facts about a population. The move from “here is what we found in our data” to “here is what is probably true more broadly” is a significant one, and it requires a different set of tools, as well as a different set of caveats.

This chapter draws that line carefully. Both branches are necessary. Neither is sufficient on its own.

2.3 Descriptive statistics

Descriptive statistics concerns itself with what is actually in front of you. It covers methods for organising, summarising, and presenting data so that it can be understood without having to read every individual observation.

Suppose a company has collected weekly sales figures from all 30 of its stores over the past year. That is 1,560 individual numbers. No one reads 1,560 numbers and comes away with a clear picture. But a table showing average weekly sales by store, a chart displaying how sales changed over the year, and a single number capturing the spread between the best and worst performers, and these make the data interpretable.

Descriptive statistics includes:

  • Measures of centre: mean, median, mode.
  • Measures of spread: range, variance, standard deviation, interquartile range.
  • Frequency distributions and histograms.
  • Graphs for categorical and numerical data.
  • Cross-tabulations and summary tables.

2.3.1 A critical point about descriptive statistics

Descriptive statistics makes no claims beyond the data at hand. If the 30 stores are all the stores the company owns, then the average you calculate is not an estimate. It is the actual average, computed from every relevant case. There is no sampling error, no margin of uncertainty, no inference involved. You are describing a complete picture.

This matters because it defines the limits of the descriptive approach. The moment you want to say something about cases not in your data, including future customers, stores not yet open, the broader market, you have moved into inferential territory.

2.4 Inferential statistics

Inferential statistics uses data from a sample to draw conclusions about a population. The word infer is exact: you are reasoning from what you can see to what you cannot see directly.

This step introduces uncertainty, and that uncertainty is not a weakness of the method. It is an honest acknowledgement of reality. You did not measure everyone. You measured some people. The gap between what you measured and what you want to know is the source of the uncertainty, and inferential statistics provides the tools to quantify it.

The main tools of inferential statistics are:

  • Estimation: using sample statistics to estimate population parameters, typically with a confidence interval that communicates the precision of the estimate.
  • Hypothesis testing: using sample data to evaluate a specific claim about a population, for instance whether a new drug works better than the old one, whether two groups differ on some measure, whether a trend is real or coincidental.

2.4.1 Why inference is necessary

Managers almost never have access to complete population data. A hotel chain wanting to know how guests feel cannot interview every person who has ever stayed. A pharmaceutical company testing a new drug cannot give it to every patient who might someday use it. A manufacturer checking product quality cannot inspect every item off the line.

In each case, a sample is taken, and the results of that sample are used to say something about the population. Done properly, this is not a compromise. Done properly, this is efficient, and the uncertainty it introduces can be measured and communicated honestly.

2.5 The two branches working together

In practice, a good analysis typically uses both branches in sequence. You begin by describing your data, computing summaries, drawing graphs, looking for patterns. This is exploratory and often reveals things that change the direction of the analysis. Then, once you have a clear sense of what the data contain, you use inferential tools to ask whether what you found is likely to reflect something real or whether it might be a product of random variation in your sample.

Consider a worked scenario. A supermarket chain has trialled a new store layout in 12 of its 200 stores over a three-month period. At the end of the trial, they compare average transaction values in the 12 trial stores against the 188 stores that kept the old layout.

The descriptive step: calculate the mean transaction value for each group, display the distributions, look for any obvious differences in the data.

The inferential step: use a two-sample t-test (covered in Chapter 23) to determine whether the observed difference in means is large enough to be unlikely under the assumption that the layout made no difference.

The descriptive step tells you what the data look like. The inferential step tells you how seriously to take what you see.

2.6 Common errors at the boundary

2.6.1 Treating a sample summary as a population fact

Suppose a company surveys 150 of its customers and finds that 62% prefer the new product design. A manager announces: “62% of our customers prefer the new design.” This statement treats an estimate as a fact. The true proportion is unknown. The sample gives an estimate, which could be stated more honestly as: “Based on 150 responses, we estimate that between 54% and 70% of customers prefer the new design”, with the range reflecting the uncertainty in the estimate.

2.6.2 Applying population data inferentially when description is sufficient

The reverse error also occurs. If you have collected data from every employee in your organisation and you want to describe something about those employees, a formal hypothesis test is not needed. The data is the population. There is nothing to infer.

This mistake tends to produce a kind of false rigour: running a t-test on data that already represents the whole group, and then reporting that the result was “statistically significant” when significance only applies to inference from samples.

2.6.3 Overgeneralising from a convenient sample

A sample collected from whoever was easiest to reach, for instance customers who filled out a feedback form voluntarily, for instance, is not representative of all customers. Inferential conclusions drawn from such a sample will be biased, often in ways that are hard to detect from the data alone.

# Descriptive vs inferential, a simple illustration
# 
# Scenario: A consulting firm tracked billable hours for a team of 8 analysts
# over the past month. These are the actual hours for all 8 analysts. These are
# IS the population (for this team, this month).

billable_hours <- c(142, 158, 135, 167, 149, 153, 140, 161)

# Descriptive: describe what we have

Because these are the actual hours for the entire team, the mean of 150.6 hours is not an estimate. It is the true team average for that month. Descriptive statistics is all that is needed.

Code 
# If instead we had data from only 8 analysts drawn from a firm of 300,
# and we wanted to estimate the firm-wide average, we would need inference.
# The sample mean becomes an estimate, and we would report it with a
# confidence interval (covered fully in Chapter 18).

n      <- length(billable_hours)
x_bar  <- mean(billable_hours)
se     <- sd(billable_hours) / sqrt(n)
t_crit <- qt(0.975, df = n - 1)

lower <- x_bar - t_crit * se
upper <- x_bar + t_crit * se

cat(sprintf("95%% confidence interval for firm-wide mean: [%.1f, %.1f] hours\n",
            lower, upper))
95% confidence interval for firm-wide mean: [141.3, 159.9] hours
Code 
cat("(If these 8 were a random sample from a larger firm)\n")
(If these 8 were a random sample from a larger firm)

The same numbers, used differently, produce two different kinds of statement. The descriptive statement is exact. The inferential statement is probabilistic and comes with a range.

2.8 Summary

Descriptive statistics summarises data that has been collected, making no claim beyond what the data directly contains. Inferential statistics uses sample data to draw conclusions about a larger population, introducing quantifiable uncertainty.

Both branches are necessary in practice. Description comes first and reveals what the data contain. Inference follows and asks how much confidence to place in what was found. The most common errors at this boundary involve presenting descriptive results as though they were established population facts, or running inferential tests on data that already cover the full population.

2.9 Key terms

Table 02.2: Key terms for Chapter 02
Term Definition
Descriptive statistics Methods for summarising and organising data that have already been collected.
Inferential statistics Methods for using sample data to draw conclusions about a population.
Parameter A numerical characteristic of a population, such as the population mean.
Statistic A numerical characteristic of a sample, used to estimate a population parameter.
Estimation Using sample data to approximate the value of a population parameter.
Hypothesis test A procedure for evaluating a specific claim about a population using sample data.
Confidence interval A range of values within which the true parameter is estimated to lie, with a stated probability.
Selection bias Systematic error arising from a sample that is not representative of the population.

2.10 Exercises

  1. A city council surveys 400 residents about satisfaction with local transport. The report states: “68% of residents are satisfied with local transport.” Is this a descriptive or an inferential statement? What would need to change to make it a properly qualified inferential claim?

  2. A department head has performance ratings for all 22 people in her team. She runs a t-test comparing the ratings of employees hired in the last two years versus those hired more than two years ago. What error is she making, and how should she approach the comparison instead?

  3. A national retailer analyses purchasing data from 50,000 loyalty card holders. Is this a sample or a population? Explain your reasoning. Does it matter for how the results should be reported?

  4. In your own words, explain why inferential statistics necessarily involves uncertainty, while descriptive statistics (applied to a complete dataset) does not.

  5. A report concludes: “Customers who received the loyalty discount spent an average of £42 per visit, compared to £38 for those who did not, a statistically significant difference.” What additional information would you need before deciding whether the loyalty discount is actually driving higher spending?
