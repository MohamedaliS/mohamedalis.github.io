16.1 Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student should be able to:

  1. Fit a simple linear regression model and interpret the intercept and slope.
  2. Use the regression equation to make predictions within the range of the data.
  3. Interpret \(R^2\) as a measure of model fit.
  4. Explain why correlation does not imply causation.
  5. Identify confounding variables and explain their role in observed associations.

16.2 Introduction

Correlation describes the strength and direction of a linear relationship. Regression goes further: it fits a specific equation to the data, allowing predictions and a more precise description of how one variable changes with another.

This chapter introduces simple linear regression, which models the relationship between one predictor variable and one response variable. It then addresses one of the most important ideas in applied statistics: the difference between finding an association and establishing a cause.

The dataset for this unit contains records for 200 employees, with variables covering training hours, productivity scores, salary, tenure, and promotion status.

16.3 Simple linear regression

Simple linear regression expresses the relationship between a response variable Y and a predictor variable X as a straight line:

\[\hat{y} = b_0 + b_1 x\]

where \(b_0\) is the intercept (the predicted value of Y when X equals zero) and \(b_1\) is the slope (the change in predicted Y for a one-unit increase in X). The hat on \(\hat{y}\) indicates that this is a predicted, not an observed, value.

The coefficients \(b_0\) and \(b_1\) are chosen to minimise the sum of squared differences between the observed values and the predicted values. This method is called ordinary least squares (OLS).

16.5 Making predictions

The regression equation can be used to predict values of Y for given values of X.

Code 
# Predicted productivity for 20, 30, and 40 training hours
new_data <- data.frame(training_hours = c(20, 30, 40))
preds    <- predict(model, newdata = new_data)

pred_table <- data.frame(
  training_hours    = new_data$training_hours,
  predicted_score   = round(preds, 1)
)

knitr::kable(pred_table,
  col.names = c("Training hours", "Predicted productivity score"),
  caption   = "Table 16.2: Predicted productivity for selected training levels") |>
  kableExtra::kable_styling(bootstrap_options = c("striped"),
                             full_width = FALSE)
Table 16.2: Predicted productivity for selected training levels
Training hours Predicted productivity score
20 59.0
30 65.6
40 72.2

Predictions are most reliable within the range of the observed training hours. Extrapolation beyond this range produces predictions the model was not designed to make and should be treated with caution.

16.6 R-squared

\(R^2\), the coefficient of determination, measures what proportion of the variation in Y is accounted for by the linear relationship with X.

\[R^2 = r^2\]

where r is Pearson’s correlation coefficient. \(R^2\) ranges from 0 to 1. A value of 0.42, for instance, means that 42% of the variability in productivity scores is accounted for by variation in training hours. The remaining 58% is due to other factors not in the model.

\(R^2\) does not tell you whether the model is appropriate or whether the relationship is causal. A high \(R^2\) simply means the line fits the data well — not that X causes Y.

16.7 Residuals

A residual is the difference between an observed value and the value predicted by the model:

\[e_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i\]

Residuals should be examined to check whether the linear model is appropriate.

A good residual plot shows points scattered randomly around zero with no obvious pattern. Systematic patterns, such as a funnel shape or a curve, indicate that the linear model may not be appropriate or that the variance is not constant.

16.8 Correlation versus causation

One of the most important principles in applied statistics is that correlation does not imply causation. Two variables can be strongly correlated for reasons that have nothing to do with one causing the other.

16.8.1 Three reasons for observed correlations

Direct causation. X causes Y. More training hours genuinely improve productivity. This is possible, but must be established by design, not just by observing a correlation.

Reverse causation. Y causes X. More productive employees may be selected for more training by their managers. If so, the causal direction is the reverse of what the regression implies.

Confounding. A third variable Z causes both X and Y. Employee tenure may drive both higher training allocations and higher productivity. The observed correlation between training and productivity may reflect the effect of tenure rather than a direct effect of training itself.

Code 
# Does controlling for tenure change the training-productivity relationship?
model_full     <- lm(productivity_score ~ training_hours + tenure_years,
                     data = df)
b1_training_alone   <- round(coef(model)[2], 3)
b1_training_adj     <- round(coef(model_full)["training_hours"], 3)
b1_tenure           <- round(coef(model_full)["tenure_years"], 3)

confound_table <- data.frame(
  Model = c("Training only", "Training + Tenure"),
  b1_training = c(b1_training_alone, b1_training_adj),
  b1_tenure   = c(NA, b1_tenure),
  R2 = round(c(summary(model)$r.squared,
               summary(model_full)$r.squared), 3)
)

knitr::kable(confound_table,
  col.names = c("Model", "Slope (training)", "Slope (tenure)", "R²"),
  caption   = "Table 16.3: Confounding: training effect with and without tenure") |>
  kableExtra::kable_styling(bootstrap_options = c("striped", "hover"),
                             full_width = FALSE)
Table 16.3: Confounding: training effect with and without tenure
Model Slope (training) Slope (tenure) R²
Training only 0.661 NA 0.345
tenure_years Training + Tenure 0.645 0.939 0.475

If the slope for training hours changes substantially when tenure is added to the model, this is evidence that tenure is a confounding variable. The apparent training effect may partly, or entirely, reflect the effect of tenure on productivity.

16.9 Establishing causation

Correlation is necessary but not sufficient for causation. Establishing causation requires:

  1. Temporal order: X must precede Y in time.
  2. Covariation: X and Y must be correlated.
  3. Elimination of alternatives: alternative explanations, including confounders, must be ruled out.

The strongest evidence for causation comes from randomised controlled experiments, where participants are randomly assigned to receive or not receive the treatment of interest. When random assignment is not possible, observational data require careful analysis and strong subject-matter knowledge to support causal claims.

16.10 Worked example

Note

Scenario. A training manager presents a regression showing that each additional training hour is associated with a higher productivity score. She concludes that the company should increase training hours for all employees. Evaluate this conclusion using the tools introduced in this chapter.

Code 
# Step 1: fit the simple model
m1 <- lm(productivity_score ~ training_hours, data = df)

# Step 2: add potential confounders
m2 <- lm(productivity_score ~ training_hours + tenure_years, data = df)
m3 <- lm(productivity_score ~ training_hours + tenure_years +
            salary_gbp, data = df)

model_compare <- data.frame(
  Model = c("Training only", "+ Tenure", "+ Tenure + Salary"),
  b_training = round(c(coef(m1)[2], coef(m2)[2], coef(m3)[2]), 3),
  R2 = round(c(summary(m1)$r.squared,
               summary(m2)$r.squared,
               summary(m3)$r.squared), 3)
)

knitr::kable(model_compare,
  col.names = c("Model", "Training coefficient", "R²"),
  caption   = "Table 16.4: Sensitivity of training effect to model specification") |>
  kableExtra::kable_styling(bootstrap_options = c("striped", "hover"),
                             full_width = FALSE)
Table 16.4: Sensitivity of training effect to model specification
Model Training coefficient R²
Training only 0.661 0.345
+ Tenure 0.645 0.475
+ Tenure + Salary 0.521 0.544

If the training coefficient remains stable across models, there is more confidence that the relationship is genuine. If it changes substantially, the simple model is likely confounded. Even a stable coefficient does not establish causation: an experiment randomising training allocation would be needed for that.

16.11 Summary

Simple linear regression fits a straight line to the relationship between a predictor and a response variable. The slope gives the change in predicted Y for a one-unit increase in X. The intercept gives the predicted Y when X is zero. \(R^2\) measures the proportion of variation in Y explained by the model.

Residuals should be examined to assess whether the linear model is appropriate. Correlation does not imply causation: an observed association may reflect direct causation, reverse causation, or confounding. Establishing causation requires temporal order, covariation, and the elimination of alternative explanations, most reliably through a randomised experiment.
